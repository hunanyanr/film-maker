# Slice-of-Life — Genre Playbook

Observational, mundane-made-magical, small moments. Not plotless — just quietly plotted.

## Core DNA
- **Specificity over plot.** The story is in the texture — the way a character makes coffee, the particular mess of a desk, the shared silence between two people.
- **Low stakes, high care.** Nothing dramatic happens, but the characters and what they notice matter profoundly.
- **Time feels real.** Scenes aren't compressed to their narrative function. They breathe. A character taking five beats to decide whether to text someone is the scene.
- **Small change is real change.** By the end, something tiny has shifted. A door has been left open. A habit broken. A moment shared.

## Act structure
Looser than other genres — slice-of-life can be a single long scene, or a day, or a season. But even the loosest piece has shape:
- The world (what the character's life is)
- The small event (the call, the meeting, the observation)
- The inner response (what it changes internally)
- The small aftermath (the thing that shows change)

## Pacing
**Patient.** Long beats. Natural rhythms. Don't cut away before the moment lands. The viewer should feel the character's time.

## Camera
Medium shots and naturalistic movement. Observational — the camera watches, doesn't dramatize. Handheld can work if it feels present rather than shaky.

## Lighting
Naturalistic. Honest window light, honest lamp light. Don't beautify — render specifically.

## Dialog
Like real speech. Overlap. Mundane. The texture of how people actually talk. A lot of non-conversation — characters exist in silence together.

## Archetypes
- The observer (often the protagonist)
- The quiet companion (present without pressing)
- The interrupter (arrives briefly, changes the rhythm)
- The absent (someone the character thinks about, writes to, waits for)

## Common pitfalls
Mistaking boring for observational. Missing the small turn (every slice-of-life needs ONE thing that changes). Beautification that flattens specificity. Plotting that overrides observation.
