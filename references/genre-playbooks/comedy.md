# Comedy — Genre Playbook

Timing, subverted expectation, escalating absurdity. The rule of three.

## Core DNA
- **Setup → expectation → subversion.** Promise one thing, deliver another. That gap is the laugh.
- **Commitment to the bit.** Characters treat absurdity with total seriousness. Half-measures kill jokes.
- **Escalation.** Each joke raises stakes. The fifth time a character gets hit with a rake is funnier than the first (if the setup earns it).
- **Specificity.** "He was hit by a car" isn't funny. "He was hit by a Miata driven by his ex-wife on her way to yoga" is.

## Act structure
Setup (normalcy, the comedic engine established) → escalation (stakes rise via increasingly absurd complications) → the moment it all goes wrong (usually the 2/3 mark) → recovery/resolution (often bittersweet — the characters have been changed, not defeated).

## Pacing
**Fast enough that jokes don't suffocate, slow enough that landings register.** Hold the reaction shot one beat longer than feels comfortable. Let the audience catch up. Don't stack punchlines.

## Camera
Medium shots (you need to see the reaction). Hold on faces after jokes. Wide for physical comedy (the body has to be visible). Cuts for rhythm.

## Lighting
Usually bright and flat for sitcom; can be anything for comedy-drama. Dark lighting often undercuts comedy — the viewer needs to read faces clearly.

## Dialog
Specific. Rhythmic. Real-sounding rather than joke-forward. Short lines land harder than long. Let silence work.

## Archetypes
- The straight man (reacts to absurdity as if it's normal, or as if it isn't, depending on tone)
- The chaos agent (brings the absurdity)
- The authority figure (fails in hilarious ways)
- The dupe (believes things too easily)

## Common pitfalls
Jokes that announce themselves. Every line a punchline (exhausting). Punching down without grace. Meta-humor that undercuts its own stakes too fast.
