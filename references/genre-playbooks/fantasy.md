# Fantasy — Genre Playbook

Mythic, enchanted, rules of magic, quest or transformation.

## Core DNA
- **Consistent magic system.** Magic has rules. Costs. Limits. Break the rules and the fiction breaks.
- **Heroic journey or transformation.** Fantasy protagonists change dramatically — either rise to meet a call, or reveal a hidden self.
- **World as character.** The setting carries weight. Specific ecology, specific customs, specific architecture.
- **Archetypes embraced.** Fantasy isn't embarrassed to use hero, mentor, trickster, shadow. Executes them with specificity rather than subverting them.

## Act structure
Setup (the world + protagonist's place in it) → call to adventure (invitation, violation, revelation) → journey/trial (quest through increasingly magical territory) → ordeal (darkest moment, faith tested) → return/transformation (protagonist returns changed, or ascends).

## Pacing
**Patient with peaks.** Fantasy can hold long on landscape and ritual. The quest has rhythm — travel, encounter, respite, travel. Each beat earns its scale.

## Camera
Wide for landscape, reverence for scale. Epic establishing shots. Intimate close-ups during character beats. Low angles for power, high for humility. Crane shots for revelation of place.

## Lighting
Heightened. Magic has its own light. Natural light dramatized — dawn, dusk, fire, moon. Rarely flat.

## Dialog
Slightly elevated register. Not Shakespearean, but considered. Characters speak with weight. Avoid modern idioms that break the spell unless intentional.

## Archetypes
- The called hero (reluctant, chosen)
- The mentor (knows, guides, often dies)
- The threshold guardian (tests worthiness)
- The shadow (the hero's dark mirror)
- The trickster (chaos, truth-through-mischief)

## Common pitfalls
Magic as plot convenience (deus ex). Generic fantasy-world (no specific ecology or culture). Dialog that announces lore. Protagonist who's special just because they're special (earn it).
