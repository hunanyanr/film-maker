# Sci-Fi — Genre Playbook

Sci-fi is speculation with consequences. One premise — one *what if* — and then the rigorous working-out of what that premise means for the characters living inside it. The best sci-fi isn't about the tech. It's about what the tech does to the people.

## Core DNA

- **One core premise.** Pick one speculative idea and commit to it. Dying space station. Last remnant of a species. Sentient object. Memory transfer. Don't pile on — one idea, deeply explored.
- **Consequences over concepts.** The idea exists to put pressure on characters. Audiences care about people, not mechanisms.
- **World logic is consistent.** Rules of the world, even when strange, behave predictably. Break your own rules and you've broken the fiction.
- **Scale + intimacy.** Vast settings (space, ruined Earth, far future) with small, specific, intimate human moments. Big frame, small heart.
- **Wonder is earned, not given.** The beautiful image means more if the character is reacting to it for the first time alongside the audience.

## Act structure

Sci-fi works well in three acts, but each act earns its escalation via the premise:

- **Setup** — Introduce the world, the rules, the character in their normal state. Quiet. Specific. The big idea is visible but not exploding yet.
- **Disruption** — Something tests the premise. A question, a discovery, a breakdown. The character has to engage with the world's logic directly.
- **Resolution** — The character either accepts, transcends, or breaks against the world's rules. The idea has done its work on them.

## Pacing

Sci-fi benefits from **held shots of scale** — long beats where the camera lingers on the environment, the strange object, the thing being revealed. Then tighter, human-scale beats for the character's response.

**Don't rush wonder.** If your character sees something impossible, hold on it. Let the viewer process alongside them.

## Camera language

- **Wide establishing shots** that communicate scale — character small in frame against the vastness
- **Long slow pushes** for revelation
- **Locked static shots** when something demands contemplation
- **Tracking shots** for exploration (character moving through an alien space)
- **Tight close-ups** on the face reacting — sci-fi lives or dies on the face reacting to wonder or threat

## Lighting + visual texture

- **Diegetic light** when possible — the console glow, the star through the viewport, the crystal's light, the pulsing core. Lets the world itself illuminate.
- **High contrast** for dramatic moments, **diffuse** for contemplative ones
- **Specific palettes per world** — a sterile station is cool grey-blue; an overgrown ruin is gold-green; a neon metropolis is magenta-cyan

## Dialog tone

- **Grounded** unless the genre is explicitly heightened (pulp space opera)
- **Jargon sparingly** — just enough to feel the world, never enough to confuse
- **Characters trust the audience** — they don't over-explain how things work
- **Silence is powerful** — a long beat of no dialog while the character processes is more sci-fi than a two-page monologue

## Character archetypes

- **The observer** — scientist, archivist, gardener, witness. Reacts to the world rather than dominating it.
- **The outsider who sees** — the child, the stranger, the non-human. Notices what insiders take for granted.
- **The last of something** — the last human, the last bird, the final witness. Their existence carries the premise.
- **The true believer vs. the skeptic** — tension between "this is wondrous" and "this is dangerous"
- **The sentient non-human** — AI, alien, engineered organism. Works best when explored with dignity, not novelty

## Emotional curve

Sci-fi often moves from **isolation → connection → loss → acceptance**. The character is alone with the premise, makes contact (with another being, with the idea itself), loses or releases, and arrives at a new kind of peace. Other curves work — sci-fi thriller has tension arc, sci-fi horror has dread arc — but the contemplative curve is the genre's native shape.

## The fable variant

Sci-fi fable is **short, specific, lands clean.** A gardener on a dying station finds a seedling. That's the whole idea. You don't need subplots. You need:

1. The world (dying station, gardener's routine)
2. The disturbance (seedling — unexpected life)
3. The relationship (gardener caring for it, understanding what it is)
4. The choice (protect it / share it / release it)
5. The resonance (final image — what it meant)

Five beats, five minutes, leaves the viewer changed. That's a sci-fi fable done right.

## Common pitfalls

- **Over-explaining the tech.** If you need a paragraph of dialog to explain how it works, your world is too cluttered.
- **Generic future-tech aesthetics.** Every station doesn't need to be sleek white. Specify. The palette is part of the idea.
- **Action scenes that could be any genre.** If you remove the sci-fi premise and nothing changes, the scene isn't earning its genre.
- **Ignoring physics/logic casually.** Break rules deliberately and own it, or honor them.
- **Skipping wonder.** The whole reason we watch sci-fi. Hold the shot. Let the music breathe. Let the character's eyes find the thing.

## When to blend

- **sci-fi + noir** → cyberpunk, detective in a future city
- **sci-fi + fable** → elemental, moral, compact (the gardener + seedling)
- **sci-fi + horror** → cosmic horror, body horror, isolation horror
- **sci-fi + drama** → grounded near-future, quiet stakes, characters-first
- **sci-fi + thriller** → high-concept action, stakes-based propulsion
