# Noir — Genre Playbook

Noir is moral ambiguity wearing a trench coat. The protagonist is compromised. The world is broken. The truth costs too much. Justice, if it arrives, arrives late and tired.

## Core DNA

- **Fatalism.** Events feel inevitable. The protagonist can't escape what they set in motion.
- **Moral compromise.** Nobody is clean. Heroes lie. Villains have reasons. The truth is uglier than the lie.
- **The past is a weapon.** Noir stories are about things that already happened catching up to the present.
- **Cities at night.** Urban, nocturnal, wet, neon, rain. The city is a character — indifferent, beautiful, predatory.
- **The femme fatale / the false ally.** There's always someone who isn't what they seem. The revelation hurts.

## Act structure

Noir doesn't do tidy three-act. It does **spiral**: each revelation pulls the protagonist deeper. What seems to resolve in Act II just opens a bigger hole.

- **Opening** — A request, a body, a question. The protagonist is hired/drawn in. The world is already broken before the story starts.
- **Descent** — Every answer is worse than the question. Allies turn out to be compromised. The protagonist discovers they're complicit.
- **Confrontation** — Truth surfaces. Often too late. The protagonist is forced to choose between bad and worse.
- **Resolution** — Pyrrhic. Something is won, but at a cost that makes the win hollow. The protagonist walks away knowing they've been changed.

## Pacing

**Slow-burn propulsive.** Held shots of a face thinking. Long silences where the viewer can hear the rain. Then sudden violence or revelation that resets the tempo. Never pacy for its own sake.

| Beat type | Typical role in noir |
|---|---|
| Establishing | Wet streets, empty corridors, a neon sign flickering. No people — the city first. |
| Reaction | The detective's face reading a letter. The femme fatale's eyes after a lie. Withhold response — make the viewer wait. |
| Dialog | Clipped, low register, subtext loaded. Characters talk about one thing while meaning another. |

## Camera language

- **Low angles** for authority figures, **high angles** for the crushed
- **Deep shadows** — figures half in darkness. Use single-source lighting (venetian blind shadows, streetlight, lamp)
- **Dutch angles** when the world tilts wrong
- **Long takes** for tension, **hard cuts** for violence or revelation
- **Rack focus** between foreground character and lurking background figure — classic noir framing

## Lighting palette

- **Teal and red** (modern noir)
- **Blue-hour ambient** with warm practical lights
- **Single-source with hard shadows** (venetian blinds classic)
- **Neon reflections on wet surfaces** (urban noir)
- Heavy contrast. Deep blacks. Small pools of light.

## Dialog tone

- Short sentences
- Subtext over text — characters don't say what they mean
- Dry, cynical voice-over (internal or narrated)
- Pauses do the work — hold on a character who chose not to speak
- Exposition is forbidden. Every line should either advance plot, complicate character, or wound

## Character archetypes

- **The compromised protagonist** — ex-cop, ex-lover, the last honest person in a corrupt system, still fighting despite knowing it's useless
- **The dangerous ally** — charming, useful, suspicious. Turns out to be pulling strings.
- **The client / the victim's proxy** — hires or asks the protagonist for help. Often has more information than they share.
- **The ghost** — the missing person, dead person, or absent presence the story orbits. Often only shown in fragments.

## Emotional curve

Opens at low ebb (tired, cynical). Brief glimmer of possibility (new case, connection to the ghost). Descent into worse and worse. Climax at the moment of choice — do the wrong thing for the right reason, or the right thing at impossible cost. Close: sadness with dignity. Not despair — recognition.

## Common pitfalls

- **Too much narration.** Noir tempts purple voice-over. Cut half of it.
- **Cliché dialog.** "This case stinks." "I'm getting too old for this." Let new lines do old work.
- **Clean endings.** Noir should leave residue. If the protagonist smiles at the end, you took the wrong exit.
- **Over-explained mysteries.** Trust the viewer to assemble what's implied. Withhold 20% of the "why."

## When to blend

- **noir + sci-fi** → cyberpunk (Blade Runner) or existential detective in an AI city
- **noir + romance** → classic femme-fatale tragedy
- **noir + soap-opera** → family secrets, generational rot, mansion-as-character
- **noir + comedy** → Coen-style, deadpan absurdity under the cynicism
- **noir + fantasy** → urban fantasy, magical detectives, occult investigations
