# Horror — Genre Playbook

Dread, reveal, psychological threat. The less you show, the more it terrifies.

## Core DNA
- **Withhold.** The monster is scariest before it's seen clearly. Once revealed, it becomes manageable.
- **Atmosphere over action.** Horror lives in held shots, ambient sound, the feeling something is about to happen.
- **Normalcy violated.** The familiar made strange. A house where things move slightly. A face in a mirror that isn't yours.
- **Character isolation.** Characters are cut off from help — physically, emotionally, or both.

## Act structure
Setup (normalcy, a crack forms) → escalation (crack widens, disbelief erodes) → confrontation (character forced to face the horror directly) → resolution (survival, consumption, or transformation — rarely clean).

## Pacing
**Slow-burn.** Most of horror is held shots of mundane things becoming wrong. Then sudden violence or revelation to reset the tempo. Never build at a constant rate — accelerate, pause, accelerate harder.

## Camera
Locked static for dread (the room that won't change). Slow push-ins toward what the character is looking at. Over-the-shoulder so the viewer sees what they see. Handheld in panic. Avoid jump cuts unless used deliberately.

## Lighting
Low-key. Single source, deep shadows. Candles, flashlights, moonlight through cracks. Embrace darkness — let the viewer's imagination fill in what the light doesn't show.

## Dialog
Minimal. When characters speak, short. Silence and ambient sound do most of the work. Whispered is better than normal-voiced.

## Archetypes
- The believer (sees it first, nobody trusts)
- The skeptic (rationalizes until forced to confront)
- The knower (elder, expert — has seen this before)
- The victim (often the skeptic, often first)
- The survivor (often the believer, transformed by what they've seen)

## Common pitfalls
Showing too much too soon. Jumpscares as substitute for dread. Ignoring sound design. Over-explained monster/threat. Survivors who are unaffected by what happened.
