# Mystery — Genre Playbook

Clue-laying, revelation, detective logic. Fair play with the audience.

## Core DNA
- **Clues are visible.** The audience must have access to every piece of evidence the protagonist does. The reveal clicks because in retrospect it was obvious.
- **Red herrings with dignity.** False leads should feel legitimate, not like authorial trickery. Each one teaches something about a character or world.
- **Investigator-forward.** The detective's mind is the engine. We follow their reasoning, not just their findings.
- **The reveal reconfigures.** Strong mysteries don't just answer whodunit — they reframe what the story was actually about.

## Act structure
Setup (the puzzle — crime, disappearance, question) → investigation (leads, interviews, dead ends, discoveries) → midpoint reversal (a key assumption breaks) → further investigation with new framing → climax (confrontation with the answer) → resolution (cost of knowing).

## Pacing
**Rhythmic.** Alternate investigation scenes (information gathering) with character scenes (detective's life, impact of the case). Don't let the plot become all machinery.

## Camera
Medium shots for interviews. Close-ups on the clue-object (the letter, the photograph, the scar). Rack focus when the detective connects something. Wide for the crime scene — orient the viewer.

## Lighting
Depends on tone — classic mystery uses warm interiors and sharp shadows; modern mystery often uses cool, forensic lighting. Either way, lighting can signal what's important (the clue lit subtly, the liar slightly shadowed).

## Dialog
Every line potentially load-bearing — something said casually in scene 2 matters in scene 8. Detective's internal voice (voice-over, or delivered to a listener) can frame the investigation.

## Archetypes
- The detective (outsider, obsessive, sees what others miss)
- The victim (often unseen — story orbits their absence)
- The suspect(s) (each with a reason, each lying about something, most lies unrelated to the crime)
- The revealer (often an overlooked figure who knows a key piece)

## Common pitfalls
Cheating the audience (clue that couldn't be seen). Over-clever twists that don't pay off emotionally. Detective who just happens to know the answer. Confessions that explain everything (let the viewer work).
